# Talk archetypes — for ideation

Most accepted conference talks fit one of six shapes. Use these to help a stuck user
find the angle in their own experience. The goal is to spark recognition ("oh, I have
one of those"), not to force a fit. A single topic can often be framed as more than one
archetype — pick the frame that plays to the strongest concrete material the user has.

## 1. The Crazy Magician
Build something incredibly cool and complex — being impractical is a feature, not a
bug. The wow factor carries the room.
- Example: running DOOM inside a VS Code chat.
- Prompt the user: "What's the most ridiculous or over-engineered thing you've built
  for fun, or always wanted to build?"
- Delivery risk to preempt: committees worry the demo will flop live. A recording or a
  fallback plan de-risks it.

## 2. War Stories
A real, lived-in struggle told honestly — the failure, the scramble, the lesson. People
connect with vulnerability and remember stakes.
- Example: dropping the production database, how you survived it, what you changed after.
- Prompt: "What's the worst incident, outage, or mistake you've lived through? What did
  it teach you?"
- Strength: inherently specific and credible — no one else has your exact story.

## 3. The Optimizer
You had a relatable efficiency problem and solved it. Highly practical; the audience
sees themselves in the problem.
- Example: how you cut AI token costs by thousands of dollars.
- Prompt: "What did you make dramatically faster, cheaper, or smaller? Do you have the
  before/after numbers?"
- Strength: concrete metrics. Push the user for the actual numbers — they're the hook.

## 4. The Deep Dive
Go under the hood of a technology everyone uses but few understand. Satisfies curiosity
and makes the audience feel smarter.
- Example: how React actually reconciles, or how a specific engine works internally.
- Prompt: "What popular tool or system do you understand deeply enough to explain what's
  really happening beneath the API?"
- Delivery risk: can get too in-the-weeds. Anchor it to why the internals matter to the
  audience's daily work.

## 5. The Oracle (future predictor)
Where the industry is heading, and how a role — developer, PM, designer — will evolve.
Provocative and shareable.
- Example: how the developer's job changes over the next few years.
- Prompt: "What shift do you see coming that most people haven't priced in yet?"
- Note: needs a defensible point of view, not just vibes. Ground predictions in what the
  user is already seeing.

## 6. Mind Reading (soft skills)
Personal growth and organizational skills that make better engineers — communication,
influence, career, process. (A talk about how to write CFPs is itself this archetype.)
- Prompt: "What non-technical skill made the biggest difference in your career, that you
  wish someone had taught you earlier?"
- Strength: broad appeal, low delivery risk. Committees like a change of pace from pure
  tech.
