# Reversim Summit CFP Guidelines

Reversim Summit (רברסים) is one of Israel's premier developer conferences — a
community conference "for developers, by developers." It runs a highly competitive,
committee-curated CFP. Use these guidelines when coaching someone submitting to Reversim.

> **Verify the live CFP each year.** Dates, formats, and limits below reflect the most
> recent known cycle (submissions via Sessionize). Confirm against the current CFP page
> before relying on any specific number, and don't fabricate details you can't find.

---

## 🎯 The Core Dynamic: Committee Selection Only (no public voting)

Selection is done **exclusively by the moderation team** — there is **no public/community
voting**. You are writing for a small group of experienced developer-reviewers, not for a
crowd scrolling proposals. Two consequences shape everything:

*   **Review is *not* blind.** Reviewers see the submitter's **name and affiliation**. If
    the speaker works for a vendor whose product is adjacent to the talk, assume a
    skeptical reader who knows exactly what that company sells — see the self-promo warning
    below. De-risk it explicitly.
*   **Moderators may reach out before deciding.** They can ask for a **detailed outline, a
    draft deck, supporting material, or a short call** — the clearer the picture of how the
    talk will actually play out, the more confident they are selecting it. This makes a
    **thorough, minute-by-minute outline the single highest-leverage asset** a submitter
    can prepare. Encourage the user to write one even if the form doesn't demand it, and to
    respond fast and engaged if a moderator makes contact.

### What this means for the proposal
*   **Title & abstract:** clear over clever. The audience (and the reviewer) "should know
    what to expect when they step into your session." Clarity is about reviewer confidence
    and audience fit, not vote-scroll appeal.
*   **Substance wins:** technical depth, feasibility, and proof the speaker can deliver.
    Concrete metrics, architecture, live demos, links to working code.
*   **Notes/outline:** use them to show the shape of the talk and reduce the committee's
    perceived risk.

## ✅ What Reversim Wants (and Explicitly Discourages)

The audience is predominantly **engineers, engineering management, and product managers**.
Submissions on all of software development are welcome — deep technical *and* adjacent
topics (product, culture, business).

**They prefer:**
*   Sessions that **appeal to a diverse audience**.
*   Sessions **based on personal experience and learnings** (your real story, not a survey).
*   **Innovative and unique** topics.

**They explicitly discourage / reject:**
*   General **HOWTOs and 101s**.
*   **Marketing and sales pitches**, and **self-promotion sessions in disguise**.
*   **Generic "how I did foo/bar with GPT"** talks. AI/GenAI is a crowded, skeptically-
    reviewed category — an AI talk must be clearly differentiated (a real result, a
    contrarian angle, a hard-won lesson), never "I pointed an LLM at a thing." A talk framed
    as doing something *without* the obvious LLM approach, or surfacing where AI fails, is
    strong counter-programming here.

---

## 🇮🇱 Israeli Developer Culture & Tone

The Reversim audience is highly technical and opinionated.
*   **Authenticity over marketing:** avoid corporate buzzwords and management jargon. They
    want real engineering pain, mistakes, and technical trade-offs.
*   **"Show me the code":** live demos, open-source repos, and hard metrics (e.g., "we cut
    latency by 40%") beat theoretical concepts.

---

## 🗣️ Language (Hebrew by default)

**The conference is held in Hebrew, and sessions are expected to be delivered in Hebrew.**
If a speaker does not speak Hebrew, the session is presented in English.
*   **Default to Hebrew** for the title, abstract, and delivery when the speaker is a Hebrew
    speaker. Match the abstract's language to the delivery language.
*   **Hebrew writing:** professional yet conversational — not overly formal/academic Hebrew,
    and not full-English transliteration. Keep technical terms in English (*RAG, LLM,
    embeddings, latent space*) inside natural Hebrew structure.
*   **English (only if not a Hebrew speaker):** polished and natural; avoid literal
    translations of Hebrew idioms.

---

## 📐 Formats & Submission Limits

*   **Full Featured Session — 30 min:** a frontal presentation. For topics with real depth.
*   **Lightning Session — 5 min:** exactly **20 slides, 15 seconds each, auto-advancing**,
    run back-to-back with no breaks. Visuals must be pre-baked; **no live demo** survives
    auto-advance. Cover exactly *one* idea or tool; fast, high-energy, highly visual. Steer
    niche topics, a cool OSS project, or a punchy story here.
*   **Up to 3 proposals per speaker**, and **up to 2 speakers per session** (name all
    speakers at submission). With a 3-proposal cap and a "you'll deliver at most one" reality,
    coach the **portfolio**: pick uncorrelated bets across different topics/formats so they
    don't compete with each other or read as spam, and de-duplicate overlapping talks.

---

## 🔁 Timeline & After Acceptance

*   **Review happens after the CfP closes**, not on a rolling basis — so "submit early to
    beat rolling acceptance" does *not* apply. Submitting early still helps: it leaves runway
    for moderator outreach (outline/deck/call) and signals engagement.
*   **First-time speakers are welcome** — moderators actively mentor new speakers from idea
    to submission and beyond.
*   **After acceptance it's a collaborative process, not a checkpoint:** early draft →
    feedback rounds with moderators → revised version → self-rehearsals → **at least one
    mandatory in-person dry-run** on pre-set dates. Accepted talks can be withdrawn if the
    speaker goes unreachable or the dry-run shows the talk isn't ready. Flag this commitment
    to the user so acceptance isn't a surprise workload.
