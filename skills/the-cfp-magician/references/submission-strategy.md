# Submission strategy & mindset

A great proposal still has to clear a noisy, human process. Coach this once the CFP
itself is in good shape — it's the difference between a good draft and an accepted talk.

## Submit early
Do not sit on a finished proposal until the deadline.
- Committees get flooded in the final days. Reviewer fatigue sets in and filtering gets
  harsher — the same abstract faces a tougher bar late than early.
- Many committees review and lock in speakers on a **rolling basis**. Slots can fill
  before the deadline even arrives.
- Early submission can also signal enthusiasm and organization to organizers.

Practical nudge: if the user has a solid draft, encourage them to submit now and refine
future submissions, rather than polishing this one for another week.

## It's a numbers game
Acceptance rates are low and rejection is the norm, even for experienced, well-known
speakers — many track a wall of "declined" with only a few "accepted" in the green.
- Reframe rejection as expected cost, not personal failure. A specific committee passing
  on a specific talk on a specific day is mostly noise.
- The winning move is volume: submit the same (lightly tailored) talk to many events.
  Encourage the user to build a short list of target conferences, not bet everything on
  one.
- Keeping a simple tracker (event, deadline, status) makes the odds visible and the
  process less discouraging.

## Beat stage fright
Fear of actually standing on stage is extremely common and stops many good talks from
ever being submitted.
- A useful trick: tell yourself you can always **decline the invitation later** if you
  get cold feet. This lowers the stakes of hitting "submit."
- In practice, once accepted, momentum and commitment usually carry people through — and
  they're glad they did it.
- If the user is anxious, normalize it, and separate the two decisions: submitting is
  cheap and reversible; deciding to actually give the talk comes later.
